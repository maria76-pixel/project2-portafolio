The problem

Financial data that couldn't be trusted

The client — a small business operating across Shopify, Amazon, and a local POS system — was losing confidence in their own financial reports. Payouts from three different platforms, each with different fee structures, payout schedules, and refund timelines, were being reconciled manually against bank statements once a month. By then, errors were weeks old and hard to trace.

The core issue wasn't missing data — it was the inability to trust the data they had. A $200 discrepancy might be a fee, a refund, a timing difference, or a genuine error. Without a system to explain the difference, every mismatch required hours of manual investigation.

Why reconciliation is complex

Three sources. Three schedules. One bank account.

  • Platform payouts group multiple orders into a single deposit — one bank line = many transactions
  • Refunds are often processed days after the original payout, creating timing mismatches
  • Platform fees (FBA, payment processing, storage) are deducted before payout — never shown as separate bank lines
  • Amazon's settlement period can delay deposits 3–5 business days after the payout report
  • Duplicate entries can appear when orders are partially refunded then re-fulfilled
System logic

The matching engine

I designed a rule-based matching system that checks each platform payout against bank deposits using three criteria in order of priority: exact amount match, amount within ±5% tolerance (to account for fees), and date proximity within a 5-business-day window. When a one-to-one match fails, the system attempts one-to-many grouping — looking for combinations of platform transactions that sum to a single bank deposit.

Each match is assigned a confidence score based on how many criteria were satisfied. Matches above 90% are auto-accepted. Below that, they enter a review queue with a plain-language explanation of why the match is uncertain.
